An Exploratory Study of Cardiac Synchrony and Joint Engagement in Parent-Child Dyads of Autistic and Neurotypical Children

Natasha Yamane1, Hannah Lansberry2, and Matthew S. Goodwin1
1 Northeastern University, Boston, MA
2 Chicago College of Osteopathic Medicine, Downers Grove, IL
INSAR 2025 Abstract ID#: 50136

Background

Research on dyadic physiological synchrony in children with autism spectrum disorder (ASD) compared to neurotypical children has yielded mixed results, generally indicating reduced or atypical synchrony in ASD. Understanding these patterns has important implications for identifying biomarkers and developing social interventions. Further research can clarify how physiological processes contribute to synchrony and how synchrony patterns vary across contexts.

Objectives

We aimed to understand the role of heart rate variability (HRV) in the physiological mechanisms underlying parent-child joint engagement (JE) by:
  1. Assessing whether parent-child dyads with and without ASD differ in HRV trend and concurrent synchrony.
  2. Investigating whether HRV trend and concurrent synchrony predict JE events during play.

Methods

We obtained behavioral and cardiac data from video and ambulatory electrocardio-graph (ECG) recordings of 11 parent-child dyads with (n = 5; M | SD: 4.1 | 1.2 years) and without ASD (n = 6; 3.3 | 1.0 years) engaged in a 15-minute dyadic play task in the enTRAIN study. The enTRAIN study was conducted to assess interpersonal physiological dynamics through semi-structured socioemotional regulation tasks and parent-report questionnaires. Each dyad received the same set of toys and was instructed to play together as they would at home, while parent and child ECGs were recorded simultaneously with the well-validated chest-worn Actiwave Cardio by CamNtech. ECG data were pre-processed into interbeat intervals used to extract HRV root mean square of successive differences (RMSSD). We then quantified HRV trend and concurrent synchrony as low- (LF) and high-frequency (HF) wavelet coherence of parent and child RMSSD, respectively. Dyadic proximity was calculated from video using three-dimensional pose estimation with popular deep learning models. Supported and Coordinated JE events were independently annotated in Datavyu by blinded, reliable raters according to Bakeman and Adamson’s (1984) State-Based Joint Engagement scheme. To assess differences in trend/LF and concurrent/HF HRV synchrony between dyads with and without ASD, we used linear mixed-effects models (LMMs), accounting for repeated HRV synchrony measures within each dyad. To examine whether parent-child HRV synchrony predicts JE outcomes, we used Bayesian mixed-effects multinomial logistic regression models (MEMLR; Eq.1) with four Markov chains and 2000 iterations, using normal priors for fixed effects and Cauchy priors for random effect standard deviations.

Results

Note: These results are from our original abstract submission and differ from those on our poster due to ongoing data analysis.
Our LMMs revealed no effect of ASD diagnostic status on either HRV trend (β = .01, p = .78) or concurrent (β = .02, p = .24) synchrony. Similarly, our MEMLR models showed no effect of ASD diagnostic status on Supported or Coordinated JE outcomes. However, greater HRV trend synchrony was associated with a reduced likelihood of Supported JE (OR = .60) and an increased likelihood of Coordinated JE (OR = 5.04). Greater concurrent HRV coherence was also associated with a reduced likelihood of Supported JE (OR = .47), but not linked to Coordinated JE. Across all models, reduced dyadic proximity was associated with increased likelihoods of both Supported and Coordinated JE.

Conclusions

Note: These conclusions are from our original abstract submission and differ from those on our poster due to ongoing data analysis.
Autistic and neurotypical toddlers do not differ in cardiac synchrony with their parents, likely due to our small sample size and limited statistical power. Across groups, closer physical proximity and HRV synchrony are associated with JE, highlighting the potential clinical significance of HRV synchrony as a dyadic physiological marker of relational attunement.
